I
The Framing

Six skills, one fused ability.

The gap — hear note → search 10 notes → find it — is six skills, not one. Scale degrees tell you which note you want. Note finding and shape memory tell you where it lives. CAGED ties chord shapes to chord tones across the entire neck. Chord-change hearing tells you what's underneath you in real time. The hero solo and song vocabulary are where everything fuses into actual playing.

Every note is measured against the tonic, not the last note you played. The tonic is always there — droning under the music — and your ear is already measuring against it instinctively. You're not learning a new sense; you're learning to consciously notice what your ear has been doing all along.

Order of Operations

Parallel & Sequential

Almost everything runs in parallel. Almost. Here's the explicit map.

Run in Parallel

Across Tracks
  • A+BScale degrees + note finding. Different parts of the brain. Both run from Day 1.
  • +CFretboard shapes. A natural minor shape needed for Wk 2 minor drone work — must start Wk 1.
  • +DChord-change hearing. Joins Wk 3 once tonic recognition has set in.
  • +FSong vocabulary. Joins Wk 5, once you have the 1, 5, and 3 of a key.
  • +EHero solo. Joins Wk 6 — analytical work needs Track A's stable tones in place.
  • ∞Transposition. Recurring 5-min micro-practice from Wk 5 onward.

Must Be Sequential

Within Each Track
  • AStable tones (1, 5, 3) before tense tones (7, 4, 2, 6). Chord tones are home base — tense tones only make sense as motion away.
  • AOne-chord drones before two-chord vamps. Tracking moving chord tones requires fixed ones first.
  • COne shape solid before adding the next. Connection only works between fluent positions.
  • DI-IV-V before adding vi. Major before minor.
  • ELearn the solo → analyze it → transpose it. Cannot be done out of order.
  • FSongs in roughly increasing complexity. Major-key clarity before minor-key blues phrasing.
The only meaningful cross-track dependency: Track E (hero solo analysis) is much more productive once Track A has reached at least the stable tones. You can learn the solo by ear before that — but the scale-degree analysis won't have anywhere to land.

IV
Track A — The Spine

Scale Degrees

Each scale degree has a fixed character — stable, tense, sweet, pulling, restless, home. Learn the seven sounds once and they apply in every key forever. This is the layer that makes "hear it → play it" possible.

End State · Wk 16Over a backing track in any common key, you can hear any in-key note and instantly know its degree. You can target chord tones (1, 3, 5, b7) when soloing, using the rest of the scale as connective tissue.
Weeks 1–2 · Hear the Tonic
Lock the key center into your ear.
  • 10 min: loop a C major drone (YouTube). Listen, hum along, let C lodge into your ear.
  • 5 min: play random notes from the C major scale over the drone. Feel how each one pulls or rests. Don't name them yet.
  • Week 2: repeat with an A minor drone, using the A natural minor shape from Track C.

The missing skill that makes everything else work. Most people skip it.

Weeks 3–5 · Stable Tones (1, 5, 3)
The chord tones. The home base of every solo.
  • Week 3 — The Root (1): drone in C, sing the root, find every C on the fretboard. The "home, arrived" feeling.
  • Week 4 — The 5th: sing G over C drone. Find every G. The "stable, hovering" character.
  • Week 5 — The 3rd: sing E over C drone. The color note — what makes major sound major. Then repeat 1-5-3 in A minor (A, E, C — note the b3 sound).
Weeks 6–9 · Tense Tones (7, 4, 2, 6)
The in-between notes that create motion.
  • Week 6 — The 7: sits a half-step under the root and pulls hard toward it. The b7 is the bluesy/Hazel sound.
  • Week 7 — The 4: suspended, leans into the 3.
  • Week 8 — The 2: light tension, easy to resolve in either direction.
  • Week 9 — The 6: wistful in major, dark in minor (b6).

Same drill each week: drone in the key, sing the degree, feel its character, find every instance on the fretboard. Use Functional Ear Trainer (free) for 5 min/day reinforcement.

Weeks 10–13 · Escape Pentatonic Purgatory
From "pool of equal notes" to chord tones with connectors.
  • Week 10: solo over an Am vamp using only chord tones (A, C, E). Three notes. Phrasing-driven. Restrictive on purpose.
  • Week 11: add b7 (G). Four notes that define blues vocabulary.
  • Week 12: add the rest of the minor pentatonic — but as passing tones between chord tones, not equal members.
  • Week 13: two-chord vamp (Am → Dm). Chord tones now move when the chord changes.
Weeks 14–16 · Integration
Solo over real progressions, naming chord-tone targets in advance.

Record yourself. Listen back. Identify which scale degree each phrase landed on. Notice when phrases resolve vs. just stop.

V
Track B — Foundation

Note Finding

Your original plan, untouched. Pure spatial recall — separate from ear training, both reinforce each other. Tools: Fretboard Trainer app, Fretuoso, Justin Guitar's note finder, or a deck of cards labeled with notes.

End State · Wk 12Any note, any string, called out at random — under 2 seconds. Past Wk 12, drop to maintenance.
Week 1
Solidify the E string.

Chromatic scale on E daily: A — A♯ — B — C — C♯ — D — D♯ — E — F — F♯ — G — G♯. 5 min/day.

Weeks 2–3
Random recall on E and A.

Someone calls "G" → you find it on E instantly → then on A. 10 min/day. Goal: no hesitation, automatic retrieval.

Week 4
Add C on all 6 strings.

Random recall: "Find C on the G string." Chromatic scale on D, G, B, high E (incorporating C).

Weeks 5–6
Add F (Wk 5), then G (Wk 6).

You now know E and A strings fully, plus C, F, G across all strings.

Weeks 7–9
Speed up — 2 notes per week.

The pattern is obvious by now. Add D and A (Wk 7), B and F♯ (Wk 8), C♯ and remaining (Wk 9).

Weeks 10–12
Full fretboard random recall.

Goal: any note, any string, under 2 seconds. After Wk 12, drop to maintenance — a few minutes daily to keep it sharp.

VI
Track C — The Bridge

Fretboard Map & CAGED

Knowing what note you want (Track A) is useless without a physical map of where it lives. This track builds that map: starting with one minor scale shape, then pentatonic boxes, then connecting them through CAGED — the system that ties chord shapes to chord tones across the entire neck.

End State · Wk 16You can target chord tones (1, 3, 5) anywhere on the neck within at least three CAGED shapes (E-shape, A-shape, D-shape) and connect at least three pentatonic boxes fluently. The fretboard feels like one map, not five disconnected boxes.

CAGED, properly explained.

The cleanest way in is something you already do: barre chords.

When you play an F barre at the 1st fret, your hand makes an E-shape — same fingering as an open E chord, with a barre instead of the nut. When you play a B barre at the 2nd fret, your hand makes an A-shape. You're already using two of the five CAGED shapes whenever you barre.

The five shapes correspond to the five open chords: C, A, G, E, D. Any chord can be played in all five shapes somewhere on the neck. A G chord:

  • G in G-shape · open position (the standard cowboy G)
  • G in E-shape · 3rd fret barre
  • G in D-shape · 7th fret area
  • G in C-shape · 10th fret area
  • G in A-shape · 12th fret area

Slide up the neck through those five shapes in order — G → E → D → C → A — and you've played a G chord five different ways across the entire fretboard. The shapes always appear in that order. C-A-G-E-D loops endlessly.

The soloing payoff: each shape contains the chord's root, 3rd, and 5th in predictable spots. In the E-shape barre, the root is on strings 6, 4, and 1. The 3rd is on string 3. The 5th is on strings 5 and 2. Once you know that pattern for one shape, you can find chord tones for any chord you're playing in that shape — anywhere on the neck.

CAGED isn't a separate scale system. It's a way of organizing chord tones across the neck using shapes you mostly already know.

Weeks 1–2 · Foundation Shape
A natural minor, one octave, in first position.

Memorize this as a physical fingering. Track A's minor drone work in Wk 2 depends on it.

  • 5th string open (A) — 5th string 2nd fret (B) — 5th string 3rd fret (C)
  • 4th string open (D) — 4th string 2nd fret (E) — 4th string 3rd fret (F)
  • 3rd string open (G) — 3rd string 2nd fret (A)

That's one octave of A natural minor. Same notes as C major, centered on A. 5 min/day.

Weeks 3–5 · Pentatonic Box 1
The standard minor pentatonic shape — solidified, with chord tones identified.

The most-played shape on guitar. Likely already in your hands; this is about knowing it, not just playing it.

  • Wk 3: play it cleanly, two notes per string, root on the 6th string (e.g., A minor pentatonic at the 5th fret).
  • Wk 4: identify every root within the box. Then every b3. Then every 5. Then every b7.
  • Wk 5: solo using only the chord tones within the box (1, b3, 5, b7). Then add the 4 as a passing tone.

This is where Tracks A and C fuse for the first time: you hear the b3 (Track A) → you know it's the b3 of the box your hand is in (Track C) → you play it.

Weeks 6–9 · CAGED Chord Tones (E-shape)
Map roots, 3rds, and 5ths within the E-shape barre.

The E-shape barre and Pentatonic Box 1 sit in the same fretboard region. They're sibling shapes.

  • Wk 6–7: for any major chord in E-shape, identify the root (strings 6, 4, 1), the 3rd (string 3), and the 5th (strings 5, 2).
  • Wk 8–9: for any minor chord in E-shape, the 3rd flattens by a half-step. Same shape, b3 instead of 3.

Drill: play the chord, then play only the roots. Then only the 3rds. Then only the 5ths.

Weeks 10–12 · Pentatonic Box 2 + A-shape CAGED
Add the next position up the neck.

The next pentatonic box sits a few frets above Box 1. The A-shape barre chord lives in the same area.

  • Wk 10: learn Box 2 cleanly, two notes per string.
  • Wk 11: identify chord tones within Box 2 — same exercise as Box 1.
  • Wk 12: A-shape CAGED chord tones — the root sits on strings 5 and 2, the 3rd on strings 4 and 1, the 5th on string 3.
Weeks 13–16 · Connect & Expand
Move fluidly between Box 1 and Box 2; introduce the D-shape.
  • Wk 13–14: connect Box 1 and Box 2 — practice phrases that move between them without breaking flow.
  • Wk 15–16: add a third box (below Box 1) and the D-shape CAGED chord tones. The fretboard starts to feel like one continuous map.

The remaining CAGED shapes (C and G) come later, naturally, once these three are fluent. Don't force them in 16 weeks.

VII
Track D — Context

Hearing Chord Changes

A chord changes underneath you. Can you identify what changed — not just "something moved"? This is what keeps you oriented when soloing over real songs.

End State · Wk 16You can listen to a Mayer or Hazel song and call out the chord changes (I-IV-V-vi in major, i-iv-v-bVII in minor) in real time, by ear, without a chart.
Weeks 3–5 · Feel the Moves Yourself
Three foundational chord movements.
  • I → IV (C → F): tonic to "lifted" sound
  • I → V (C → G): tonic to "tense, wants-to-resolve" sound
  • I → vi (C → Am): tonic to "sadder version of home"

Play these on guitar yourself first. Feel each move's character before training your ear to recognize them passively.

Weeks 6–8 · I, IV, V Recognition (App-Based)
80% of pop, rock, and blues lives here.

Use Functional Ear Trainer's chord-recognition mode. 5 min/day in the app, plus 5 min on guitar testing yourself with backing tracks.

Weeks 9–11 · Add the vi (the "sad" chord)
Four chords behind half of all popular music.

Drill: put on a song, call out the changes in real time as I / IV / V / vi.

Weeks 12–13 · Minor Key Changes
i, iv, v (or V), bVII, bVI.

Hazel and Mayer both use a lot of minor-key movement. Train this specifically.

Weeks 14–16 · Real Songs
Where it all fuses.

Pick Mayer/Hazel songs. Listen, identify the changes by ear, verify with chord charts. You hear the chord change → you know which chord tones just became active → you know where they live → your scale-degree ear tells you which one to target.

VIII
Track E — Deep Application

Hero Solo

The deep lab. One solo, learned by ear, then dissected through scale degrees and CAGED, then transposed to multiple keys.

End State · Wk 16You can play your hero solo cleanly in the original key and at least two other keys, using scale-degree thinking (not hand patterns) to transpose. You can name the function of every note in the solo.
i.
Slow Dancing in a Burning Room
John Mayer
Clean melodic logic, clear chord-tone targeting. Best entry point.
ii.
Gravity
John Mayer
Acoustic clarity, heavy use of major 3rds and 4ths. Strong second choice.
iii.
Maggot Brain (intro 60–90s)
Eddie Hazel
Just the opening minute. Iconic, soulful, blues-based phrasing study.
Weeks 6–8 · Learn It By Ear
Tabs only as backup.

Don't memorize shapes — understand notes. Where does each phrase start? End? Where does the bend live?

Weeks 9–10 · Analyze Through Scale Degrees + CAGED
Real-world theory work.
  • What scale degree does each phrase start on? End on?
  • Which CAGED shape is his hand in for each phrase?
  • When he bends, what degree → what degree?
  • Where are the chord tones vs. passing tones?
Weeks 11–12 · Transpose
Two new keys. Use scale-degree thinking, not hand patterns.

This is the test of whether Track A is actually working: hear the function → know the location in the new key → play it.

Weeks 13–16 · Add a Second Solo
Same process, faster — the framework is in place.

You're not learning the solo; you're learning how he thinks.

X
Recurring Micro-Practice

Transposition Practice

Not a track — a recurring 5-minute warmup or cooldown. The most direct test of whether scale-degree thinking is actually working in your hands. Once you can transpose freely, you've internalized the thinking.

End State · Wk 16Take any short melody you know and play it in any key on demand, by hearing the intervals/degrees rather than calculating fret math.
i.
Simple Melodies
Nursery rhymes, well-known riffs (Happy Birthday, Twinkle Twinkle, Smoke on the Water). Transpose to one new key.
ii.
Any Key on Demand
Same melodies, but now transpose to any key without warning.
iii.
Song Phrases
Transpose phrases from songs you're learning in Track F.
iv.
Hero Solo
The full Track E transposition exercise — multiple keys, scale-degree thinking only.

Start at Stage I in Wk 5. Move up a stage roughly every 3–4 weeks. 5 min/day as a warmup or cooldown — never the centerpiece of a session.

XIII
Honest Notes

Priorities & Permissions

If a week feels overwhelming

Drop in this order: F → D → E → C. Never drop A or B.

Track F (songs) is the volume layer — skipping a song cycle costs you nothing structural. Track D (chord changes) can pause for a week or two. Track E (hero solo) is precious but can wait. Track C is the bridge — losing it for a week is recoverable.

Tracks A (scale degrees) and B (note finding) are the spine. If those fall, the rest doesn't have anything to attach to. Protect them.

A note on phrasing
You're handling technique in parallel — but the hero solo IS phrasing work.

Three notes with great phrasing sound like Hazel. Fifty correct notes with no phrasing sound like an exercise. The theory tells you which notes; phrasing makes them mean something. Don't let one lap the other.

A note on the ear
You have ten years of instincts. Trust them when they push back.

If a week's drill feels wrong in your hands, that's data. Adjust the plan, not your ear.

A note on the timeline
Week numbers are scaffolding, not gospel.

If a stage isn't sticking, repeat the week. If it's clicking faster than expected, push ahead. The plan exists to serve the practice, not the other way around.
